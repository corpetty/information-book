A working draft of a book about why complex ideas have such a hard time traveling through modern media — and what can be done about it.

## The problem the book takes on

Something has shifted in how ideas move through our information environment. Forty years ago, a complex finding could take months to reach a general audience, and the journey had structural constraints: it had to survive peer review, journalism, popularization, conversation. Each of those filters distorted the finding, but the filters were also slow enough to function as quality checks. Today the filters have collapsed. Ideas can reach millions of people in hours, and the dynamics that decide *which* ideas spread reward emotional charge over accuracy.

The result is an information environment where most of what reaches people is either oversimplified to the point of being wrong, optimized to provoke a reaction, or manufactured to look like a finding without being one. This isn't a story about bad actors. It's a story about a pipeline whose structural constraints have been removed without the corresponding work to replace them.

## The book's diagnosis

Ideas move from reality to people through a sequence of stages: raw observation, formal finding, news, public discussion, meme. At every stage, two things happen at once.

**Transport** is how the idea is re-encoded for the next stage — compressed, simplified, translated for a wider audience. Transport is lossy: each compression strips something, and what gets stripped is usually the *decoding instructions* that came with the original (the methodology, the qualifications, the scope) rather than the bare claim itself. The claim arrives intact and the receiver decodes it with whatever instructions they already have, which is often the wrong ones.

**Selection** is the gate at each stage that decides which ideas pass through at all. Every gate has criteria — what's publishable, what's newsworthy, what's shareable, what's clickable — and those criteria are set by the *medium* the gate runs in. In modern digital media, the gate-criteria reward emotional charge and identity signal, not truth-correlation. Selection is the dominant force.

Most accounts of the modern information environment focus on one of these two mechanisms. The book argues they operate in parallel and you cannot understand the problem by holding only one.

## What the book argues

Three working conclusions hold the structure together:

1. The medium *is* the selection criteria. Changing the medium changes which ideas can win, which makes the medium the upstream lever for everything else.

2. Truth survives compression only when the receiver's decoding key matches the original — and most of the time it doesn't, so the same compressed claim can land as preserved truth, inverted truth, or as a pure identity signal depending on who's reading it.

3. The prescription is not to fragment into smaller networks (they have their own problems) but to build *bridges* between networks that don't share preconditions — bridges staffed by deep specialists who have also been trained in cross-domain pattern-recognition, and held by institutional infrastructure that survives polarized trust.
